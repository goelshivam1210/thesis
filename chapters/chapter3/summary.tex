\section{Summary and Future Directions}
\label{sec:benchmarks_summary}

This chapter presented two benchmark environments for developing and evaluating agents that operate under open-world novelty. NovelGridworlds introduced a structured novelty taxonomy,
% cross-paradigm evaluation through a socket interface,
and metrics that capture both performance degradation and novelty detection. NovelGym extended this foundation with a formal environment representation, composable novelty transformations, a modular architecture supporting hybrid planning-learning agents, and an evaluation protocol with adaptation-focused metrics. Together, these environments provide the experimental infrastructure for the adaptive methods developed in the subsequent chapters of this dissertation.

The experimental results across both environments reveal consistent themes. Planning agents detect novelties reliably through discrepancies between expected and observed state transitions but lack mechanisms for adapting their models in response. Reinforcement learning agents can potentially adapt through continued interaction but require substantially more experience and are brittle when novelties alter the observation space or introduce catastrophic states. Hybrid agents that integrate planning with learning-based recovery consistently achieve faster adaptation, as measured by $T_\text{adapt}$, because they decompose the problem and focus learning on the specific subgoal affected by the novelty. However, the failure-driven nature of hybrid adaptation has a blind spot: beneficial novelties that do not cause execution failures, such as the chest shortcut, go undetected and unexploited.

The running example of the axe novelty---in which trees become unbreakable unless the agent acquires and uses a new tool---illustrates these tensions concretely. A planning agent detects the failure of the {\tt break} operator but cannot learn the recovery behavior. A reinforcement learning agent can eventually learn to use the axe but requires orders of magnitude more experience. A hybrid agent localizes the failure and learns a targeted recovery policy. In \cref{ch:rapid_learn}, we develop the RAPid-Learn framework, which formalizes and extends this hybrid recovery mechanism, using the axe novelty and other NovelGym scenarios as primary evaluation domains.

Several directions for future work emerge from the benchmarking framework introduced in this chapter.

\paragraph{Multi-agent open-world settings.} NovelGym's architecture, built on PettingZoo, already supports multi-agent environments, but the evaluation in this chapter focused on single-agent scenarios. Extending the framework to fully multi-agent open-world settings---where novelty can originate from other agents' changing strategies or capabilities---would open a qualitatively different research frontier. The transformation formalism introduced in \cref{sec:ng_novelty} naturally extends to such settings by defining agent-behavioral transformations alongside environmental ones, and the evaluation metrics can be generalized to capture coordination robustness and collective adaptation. This extension is particularly timely given the emergence of LLM-based multi-agent systems~\citep{wang2024survey,ahn2022can,huang2022language}, which face distinctive challenges under novelty---hallucination of infeasible actions, brittleness to unexpected tool changes, and difficulty maintaining coherent plans---but lack standardized benchmarks for evaluating their open-world novelty handling capabilities.

\paragraph{Goal-directed curiosity for novelty handling.} The experimental results in this chapter reveal that curiosity-driven exploration, as implemented through ICM~\citep{pathak2017curiosity}, provides an imperfect signal for novelty adaptation: the forward model's prediction error correlates with state novelty but not necessarily with task relevance. Recent work by \citet{lorang2025curiosity} takes a promising step by combining ICM-driven exploration with symbolic operator learning and reward machines, but the curiosity signal itself remains undirected. A still-open research direction is the development of goal-conditioned or task-aware curiosity mechanisms~\citep{liu2022goal} that shape exploration bonuses by the agent's current subgoal or by discrepancies between planned and observed operator effects. In hybrid planning--learning systems, the symbolic planner provides a natural source of such goal structure. The benchmark environments introduced in this chapter, with their structured novelties spanning beneficial, detrimental, and irrelevant categories, provide a natural testbed for evaluating such strategies.

\paragraph{Toward physically grounded open-world simulation.} The environments presented in this chapter operate in discrete gridworlds, which provide the controlled abstraction necessary for systematic evaluation but lack the physical complexity of real-world robotic settings. Recent work on open-world simulation platforms, such as Virtual Community~\citep{zhou2025virtual}, demonstrates the feasibility of physics-grounded environments that combine robotic manipulation, human-robot interaction, and large-scale scene generation in realistic 3D worlds. Bridging NovelGym's structured novelty injection framework with the physical fidelity of such platforms would enable evaluation of how novelty-aware agents cope with continuous state spaces, contact dynamics, and perceptual uncertainty---challenges that are central to the robotic interaction methods developed in \cref{ch:generalization}.
