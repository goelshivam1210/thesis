\section{Thesis Outline}
\label{sec:outline}

The remainder of this dissertation is organized as follows.

Chapter 2 presents background and related work relevant to open-world autonomy. It discusses the limitations of closed-world assumptions in contemporary artificial intelligence and robotics systems and reviews prior work in task and motion planning, reinforcement learning, novelty-aware agents, and robotic manipulation. The chapter establishes the conceptual foundations used throughout the dissertation.

Chapter 3 introduces benchmark environments designed to systematically introduce novelty and evaluate agent behavior under open-world conditions. These environments provide mechanisms for controlled novelty injection and enable rigorous evaluation of how agents detect and respond to unexpected changes in their environment.

Chapter 4 develops adaptive planning–learning systems that allow agents to recover from execution failures during task execution. The chapter presents approaches that integrate planning mechanisms with learning-based adaptation, enabling agents to detect when previously learned models become insufficient and modify their behavior in response to unexpected situations.

Chapter 5 investigates how robots can acquire interaction policies that generalize across objects and tasks. It presents object-centric and force-based reinforcement learning approaches for manipulation tasks involving sustained contact and complex object dynamics, demonstrating how these methods enable robots to learn interaction strategies that transfer across diverse environments and manipulation scenarios.

Chapter 6 concludes the dissertation by summarizing the main findings and discussing future directions for research on autonomous agents and robots operating in open-world environments.
