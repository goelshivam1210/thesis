\section{Thesis Contributions}

This dissertation develops methods for enabling autonomous agents and robots to operate effectively in open-world environments through learning, adaptation, and generalization. The contributions address how such systems can be evaluated under novelty, how they can adapt when execution failures occur, and how robots can acquire interaction policies that generalize across objects, tasks, and robotic embodiments.

The main contributions of this dissertation are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}

\item It introduces benchmark environments and evaluation methodologies that systematically introduce novelty into agent environments, enabling rigorous study of autonomous systems operating under open-world conditions. These environments provide mechanisms for controlled novelty introduction and support evaluation of how agents detect, respond to, and adapt to unexpected changes in their environment.

\item It develops adaptive planning–learning systems and architectural frameworks that enable agents to recover from execution failures in changing environments. By integrating planning mechanisms with learning-based adaptation, these systems allow agents to detect when previously learned models become insufficient and modify their behavior to continue task execution despite unexpected changes.

\item It introduces object-centric reinforcement learning approaches for robotic interaction that learn manipulation policies in force space. These methods enable robots to acquire interaction strategies that generalize across objects, tasks, and robot embodiments, supporting manipulation behaviors involving sustained contact and diverse physical interaction dynamics.
\end{itemize}
