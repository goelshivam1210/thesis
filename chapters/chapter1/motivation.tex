Autonomous agents and robots are increasingly expected to operate in real-world environments such as homes, warehouses, hospitals, and urban spaces. In these settings, agents must interact with diverse objects, perform varied tasks, and respond to changing environmental conditions. Unlike controlled laboratory settings, real-world environments are inherently open: new objects may appear, interaction dynamics may change, and tasks may evolve in ways that were not anticipated during system design or training. Developing systems that can operate robustly under such conditions remains a central challenge in artificial intelligence and robotics.

Most contemporary approaches are developed under closed-world assumptions, where the environment, task structure, and interaction dynamics encountered at deployment closely match those observed during training. These assumptions enable effective learning and planning, but they limit the ability of agents to operate reliably when confronted with novelty. In open-world settings, even small deviations from prior experience can invalidate learned models, cause action execution to fail, and degrade policy performance. As a result, agents must be able to remain effective when their existing knowledge becomes incomplete or incorrect.

Two fundamental challenges arise in this setting. First, when novelty leads to execution failure, an agent must be able to adapt its behavior efficiently, acquiring only the knowledge necessary to restore successful task execution without relearning from scratch. Second, even in the absence of failure, agents must be able to generalize their learned behaviors across variations in objects, environments, and embodiment, so that previously acquired skills remain applicable under new conditions. Addressing both adaptation and generalization is essential for enabling robust operation in open-world environments.

A key difficulty in achieving these capabilities lies in how agents represent their interaction with the world. When representations are tightly coupled to specific environments or control strategies, failures induced by novelty are difficult to interpret, and learned behaviors do not transfer beyond the conditions in which they were acquired. In contrast, representations that capture meaningful structure in tasks, actions, and interactions can support both targeted adaptation and reuse across variations. In particular, structured abstractions can expose where mismatches occur during execution, restrict learning to the components that must change, and encode invariance that allow behaviors to generalize across different contexts.

This dissertation investigates the role of structured abstractions in enabling agents to operate effectively under novelty. We begin by developing formal frameworks, taxonomies, and benchmark environments that enable systematic study of novelty as a mismatch between an agent’s internal models and the environment. Building on this foundation, we examine how abstractions over actions enable efficient adaptation when execution fails, and how abstractions over interaction enable the learning of skills that generalize across objects, dynamics, and robotic embodiment.

Taken together, these contributions advance a unified perspective: while novelty introduces unavoidable mismatch in open-world environments, the structure of an agent’s representations determines whether this mismatch leads to brittle behavior or can be managed through efficient adaptation and generalizable skill learning. By organizing interaction at appropriate levels of abstraction, agents can localize failure, acquire new behaviors when needed, and reuse existing knowledge across diverse conditions.
