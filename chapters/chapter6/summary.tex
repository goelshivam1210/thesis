\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch6_summary}

This chapter situated the contributions of this dissertation within the broader landscape of open-world autonomy research. We reviewed prior work across the stages of the perceive--plan--execute loop---sensing and perception, planning and learning, and execution and recovery---and identified the recurring challenges that motivate the structured abstraction approach developed in this dissertation.

The discussion of foundation models highlighted that while large-scale pretraining substantially expands the range of situations an agent can handle, fundamental challenges remain in diagnosability, physical grounding, reliability, and sample-efficient adaptation. The abstraction-based perspective developed in this dissertation addresses these challenges through complementary mechanisms: structured action abstractions that enable failure localization and targeted learning, and interaction abstractions that capture task-relevant physical invariants. These approaches are not alternatives to foundation models but potential complements, and their integration represents a promising direction for building agents that combine the breadth of large-scale learning with the efficiency and interpretability of structured representations.

The next and final chapter summarizes the main contributions of this dissertation and discusses future directions for research on autonomous agents and robots operating in open-world environments.
