\chapter{Related Work and Perspectives on Open-World Autonomy}
\label{ch:perspectives}

The preceding chapters have developed three contributions toward enabling agents and robots to operate in open-world environments: benchmark environments for systematically evaluating agents under controlled novelty (\cref{ch:benchmarks}), action abstractions that enable efficient adaptation when execution fails (\cref{ch:rapid_learn}), and interaction abstractions that support generalization across objects, physical parameters, and robotic embodiments (\cref{ch:generalization}). Each of these chapters included focused discussions of related work specific to its technical contribution.

This chapter steps back to situate these contributions within the broader landscape of open-world autonomy research. We review prior work organized around the stages of the perceive--plan--execute loop that has served as a recurring conceptual framework throughout this dissertation, and discuss how the field has evolved---particularly with the emergence of large-scale foundation models that are reshaping the design space for autonomous agents. We then examine how the contributions of this dissertation relate to and complement these broader developments, and identify open problems that remain.
