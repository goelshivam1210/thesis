\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
Autonomous agents and robots deployed in real-world environments must operate under open-world conditions in which unforeseen changes, novel objects, and unexpected task variations are inevitable. However, most contemporary artificial intelligence and robotics systems are developed under closed-world assumptions where environments, tasks, and dynamics encountered during deployment closely match those observed during training. These assumptions limit the ability of intelligent systems to adapt when prior knowledge becomes insufficient and to generalize robustly across new situations.
This dissertation develops methods for enabling autonomous agents and robots to operate effectively in open-world environments through learning, adaptation, and generalization. To this end, it introduces benchmark environments that systematically inject novelty and enable rigorous evaluation of novelty-aware agents. It further develops adaptive planning-learning systems that integrate task and motion planning with reinforcement learning-based recovery mechanisms, allowing agents to detect execution failures and adapt their behavior when previously learned knowledge becomes insufficient. Finally, it demonstrates how robots can acquire generalizable interaction capabilities through object-centric and force-based reinforcement learning policies that enable sustained-contact manipulation across diverse objects and tasks.
Together, these contributions provide a unified perspective on building autonomous agents and robots capable of operating in open-world environments. By combining benchmark-driven evaluation, adaptive planning-learning systems, and generalizable embodied interaction policies, this work advances the development of intelligent systems that can robustly operate in complex, dynamic, and evolving real-world settings.
